Your income,

For many the path is blocked — for some the path is full of twists and turns. This already difficult path grew even harder when the Supreme Court handed states the power to ban abortion last summer.

United States of Abortion Bans

A Right Removed

On June 24, 2022, the Supreme Court overturned 50 years of abortion protections and precedent in their ruling in Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization. At least 13 states were waiting to pull the trigger.

As people across the country scrambled, lawmakers in these states waited, knowing that abortions would soon be banned within their borders.

People in Louisiana woke up on June 23 with abortion as an option. 24 hours later, it was illegal. Three days later it was legal again. Eleven days later: illegal. Four days later: legal. Seventeen days later: illegal.

As this back-and-forth battle for abortion access played out in the courts, Nancy was caught in the crossfire.

In August 2022, Nancy learned that the fetus she was carrying had a rare and fatal condition where the fetus does not form a skull. When she tried to go to an abortion clinic, it had shut down. When she tried to go to a hospital, the doctors there weren’t sure if they were allowed to help her.

Nancy told the New York Times: “They threw me to the wolves. You’re telling me all this is wrong with the baby, but, ‘OK, figure it out on your own.’”

In the months after the Supreme Court decision, similar stories played out again and again across the U.S.

People sought abortion care because their pregnancy was not viable, because they were still a teenager, because they couldn’t afford another child, because being pregnant didn’t match their gender identity, because they were raped, because their own health was at risk, because they simply didn’t want to be pregnant.

Regardless of their reason, abortion should have been an option. But it wasn’t — they were left to “figure it out on their own.”

These names represent a fraction of the thousands of people distressed after trigger bans went into effect. Countless other stories are unknown. Data cannot account for the number of people who had abortions outside of the health care system or who remained pregnant.

WeCount, a national abortion reporting effort from the Society of Family Planning, found that there were 32,260 fewer reported abortions in the months following the court's June decision, compared to the period before.

States with more people living below the poverty line and the weakest maternal support systems had the greatest decline in abortions. The WeCount report stated: “the impact of the Dobbs decision is not equally distributed.”

Even with these inequities, some state lawmakers callously ignored them, introducing ban upon ban upon ban, further curtailing abortion access in communities that already struggled to access basic health care. After the initial effects of trigger bans, even more states moved to outlaw abortion.

Many states also continued to dismantle access in other ways.

State legislatures in Arizona, South Carolina, and Indiana, where abortion is still legal, but highly restricted, banned using telehealth to obtain abortion medication. In defiance of these laws, one clinic in Phoenix began mailing pills to a California border town for its Arizona patients to pick up.

Multiple states, including Ohio and West Virginia, passed laws granting funds to anti-abortion centers and fake reproductive health clinics that call themselves Crisis Pregnancy Centers — although often there is no medical staff and patients are deliberately fed misinformation about abortion and religious propaganda.

Wyoming lawmakers passed the nation’s first complete ban on medication abortion, prohibiting the prescription, sale and use of abortion pills. The law was set to take effect on July 1, 2023, but was blocked by a district judge.

And Montana’s legislature, in an effort to go around the state’s constitutional protections, passed laws mandating ultrasounds before all abortions; restricting public money going to abortions; banning surgical abortion, the most common method to terminate pregnancy in the second trimester; and targeted regulation of abortion providers (TRAP) laws, or requirements that are medically unnecessary under the guise of "patient safety." All these laws were blocked from taking effect by a Montana judge.

Hundreds of bills restricting abortion were introduced in 2022 and 2023 to date, despite guidelines from the World Health Organization that say removing unnecessary policy barriers facilitates safer abortion access.

Although some of these laws have been enjoined or struck down by the courts, it’s clear that state legislatures pushed through anti-abortion bills despite the will of the people. In the latest Gallup poll from May 2023, 85% of Americans believed abortion should be legal under some or all circumstances. And there is no state in the country where a total ban on abortion is popular.

The People Push Back

Outside the reproductive rights movement, few expected the overturn of Roe v Wade. Then the unthinkable happened.

Thousands of people across the country took to the streets — some organized, some impromptu — with chants of “We Won’t Go Back!” and “Bans Off Our Bodies.”

The anger was palpable, reaching the steps of the U.S. Supreme Court and the front yards of conservative Justices Samuel Alito and Clarence Thomas. In Arizona, protestors banged on glass doors and forced the state senate to call a recess.

The fight was justified.

A few months later in September 2022, Sen. Lindsey Graham introduced a nationwide abortion ban, making it clear that giving states the power to regulate abortion was never the goal — they wanted to take away all access.

It would become clear this assault on abortion was part of a larger strategy targeting freedoms of all kinds, including marriage equality, contraception, gender-affirming care and more.

They said the quiet part out loud. But the people were listening.

Kansas was a lynch pin. It was the first state to vote directly on the issue of abortion since abortion rights were stripped in June 2022. In August 2022, voters there soundly rejected a proposed state constitutional amendment that would have said there was no right to an abortion in the state.

It was a surprising victory to many people in the deeply conservative state. "It's never looked like this in Kansas.," 23-year-old Jae Moyer told NPR, "It's so amazing. I'm so proud of my state right now."

And Kansas wasn’t a blip. Every time the question of abortion was put directly to the people, voters chose to support greater access, not less.

The Mazes

In the year since the courts allowed states to ban abortion, the path to abortion has become more and more complicated.

For each state where there is a path to abortion, we built a maze capturing how difficult it is to get care. For the states with bans, there is no viable path through the state. The complexity of each maze was calculated using 56 different data points which were last updated on July 21, 2023. (read more about these in the methodology): the more abortion bans and restrictions, the more complex the maze; the more abortion protections, the less complex the maze.

Now more than ever, getting an abortion depends on where you live, how much money you make, your race, ethnicity, and age, your access to health care, and so many other compounding factors.

These mazes serve as a starting point, tracing all the political, policy, and structural barriers to abortion, but they cannot capture the emotional and physical toll. For as complex as some of these mazes are, the actual path to abortion can be even harder.

Note: The complexity for each state maze was calculated using 56 state-level metrics, including the ones mentioned above. The metrics were last updated July 21, 2023 and represent a snapshot in time. The mazes may not represent the current ever-evolving abortion landscape in each state due to ongoing legal challenges, court rulings, and new legislation.

Methodology

To calculate the complexity of each state maze, 56 state-level data points were collected, normalized, weighted, and combined to produce a complexity score. Abortion bans were weighted at 50, with ban exceptions weighted at 3. Other policies and laws aimed at limiting abortion access were weighted at 2. Relevant health, political, and public opinion metrics were weighted at 1.

The metrics were last updated July 21, 2023 and represent a snapshot in time. The mazes may not represent the current ever-evolving abortion landscape in each state due to ongoing legal challenges, court rulings, and new legislation.

Mazes were generated using a depth first search algorithm, with a size and difficulty that matched the complexity score.
