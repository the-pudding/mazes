A Right Removed

Even before the Supreme Court ended abortion rights at least 13 states were waiting to pull the trigger.

On May 2, the Supreme Court‚Äôs upcoming ruling in Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization was leaked ‚Äî they were overturning 50 years of abortion protections and precedent.

WeCount, a national abortion reporting effort from the Society of Family Planning, found that there were 32,260 fewer reported abortions in the months following the court's June decision, compared to the period before.

States with more people living below the poverty line and the weakest maternal support systems had the greatest decline in abortions: ‚Äúthe impact of the Dobbs decision is not equally distributed.‚Äù

Even knowing these inequities, some states callously ignored them, introducing ban upon ban upon ban, further curtailing abortion access in communities that already struggled to access basic health care. After the initial effects of trigger bans, even more states moved to outlaw abortion.

Many states also began to dismantle access in other ways: Florida passed a law that prohibits the mailing of medication abortion pills.

Arkansas, Montana, and Utah passed laws around the targeted regulation of abortion providers (TRAP), or requirements that are medically unnecessary under the guise of "patient safety." The World Health Organization guidelines state that removing these unnecessary policy barriers facilitates safe abortion access.

Ohio and West Virginia passed laws to fund to anti-abortion centers and fake reproductive health clinics that call themselves Crisis Pregnancy Centers ‚Äî although often there is no medical staff and patients are deliberately fed misinformation about abortion and religious propaganda.

Close to 1,000 bills restricting abortion were introduced in 2022 and 2023 to date. In 2023 alone, Montana introduced over 30 bills to curtail abortion.

The People‚Äôs Power

Outside the reproductive rights movement, few expected the overturn of Roe v Wade. Then the unthinkable happened.

Thousands of people across the country took to the streets ‚Äî some organized, some impromptu ‚Äî with chants of ‚ÄúWe Won‚Äôt Go Back!‚Äù and ‚ÄúBans Off Our Bodies.‚Äù

The anger was palpable, reaching the steps of the U.S. Supreme Court and the front yards of conservative Justices Samuel Alito and Clarence Thomas. In Arizona, protestors banged on glass doors and forced the state senate to call a recess.

The fight was justified.

A few months later in September 2022, Republican Sen. Lindsey Graham introduced a nationwide abortion ban, making it clear that giving states the power to regulate abortion was never the goal ‚Äî they wanted to take away all access.

It would become clear this assault on abortion was part of a larger strategy targeting freedoms of all kinds, including voting rights, marriage equality, contraception, gender-affirming care and more.

They said the quiet part out loud. But the people were listening.

Kansas was a lynch pin. It was the first state to vote on abortion since abortion rights were stripped in June 2022. In August 2022, voters there soundly rejected a proposed state constitutional amendment that would have said there was no right to an abortion in the state.

It was a surprising victory to many people in the deeply conservative state. "It's never looked like this in Kansas.," 23-year-old Jae Moyer told NPR, "It's so amazing. I'm so proud of my state right now."

And Kansas wasn‚Äôt a blip. When abortion was on the ballot, voters chose to support greater access, not less.

The momentum continued.

In April 2023, voters upended 15 years of Republican control of the Wisconsin Supreme Court by electing liberal judge Janet Protasiewicz, pushing the court‚Äôs balance to the left and offering a pathway to strengthen abortion access in the state.

Fellow Wisconsin Supreme Court Justice Jill Karofsky told the Milwaukee Journal Sentinel that the results left her speechless. Karofsky then added: "I am so happy for the people, I am so happy for the state of Wisconsin. I think they spoke loudly‚Ä¶‚Äù

These election results starkly illustrated the longstanding divide between state lawmakers who were working to limit abortion access and voters who wanted to protect, expand, and enshrine it.

It also gave the people a roadmap of sorts to help protect and expand access, a glimmer of hope, a confirmation of their power.

In the year since the courts allowed states to ban abortion, the path to abortion has become more and more complicated.

To try to capture this, we built 51 unique mazes, representing each of the 50 states and Washington D.C. The complexity of each maze is calculated using over 50 different data points (read more about these in the methodology): the more abortion bans and restrictions, the more difficult the maze; the more abortion protections, the less difficult the maze.

Now more than ever, getting an abortion depends on where you live, how much money you make, your race, ethnicity, and age, your access to health care, and so many other compounding factors.

These mazes serve as a starting point, tracing all the political, policy, and structural barriers to abortion, but they cannot capture the emotional and physical toll. For as complex as some of these mazes are, the actual path to abortion can be even harder.

Methodology

To calculate the difficulty of each state maze, 56 state-level data points were collected, normalized, weighted, and combined to produce a complexity score. Abortion bans were weighted at 50, with ban exceptions weighted at 3. Other policies and laws aimed at limiting abortion access were weighted at 2. Relevant health, political, and public opinion metrics were weighted at 1.
